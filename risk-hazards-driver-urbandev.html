Poorly planned and managed urban development

Whether or not disaster risk is factored into investment decisions in urban development will have a decisive influence on the future of DRR, which will largely be played out in city regions.

A new wave of urbanisation is unfolding in hazard-exposed countries and with it, new opportunities for resilient investment emerge.

City regions are becoming increasingly exposed, particularly in Asia , and are creating new patterns of intensive risk (UNISDR, 2013). Expected annual average losses from earthquake and cyclone wind damage alone represent approximately US$180 billion per year (UNISDR, 2013) (UNISDR, 2013). At the same time, the transformation of city regions, through badly planned and managed urban development and environmental modification, has generated new hazards and extensive risks that now are responsible for comparable, additional levels of loss (UNISDR, 2013). For instance, increased run-off from a growing area of impermeable surfaces, inadequate investment in drainage and water management and the development of low-lying flood prone areas can generate increased flood risk (UNISDR, 2013).

Disaster risk may be increasing faster in rapidly growing small- and medium-sized urban centres than in either rural areas or larger cities.(UNISDR, 2011)

Historically, much urban growth in low and middle-income countries has occurred through informal mechanisms of land acquisition, building and infrastructure provision. Mortality and extensive risks are disproportionately concentrated in these countries, where a large proportion of low-income households lives in informal settlements in hazard-exposed areas (UNISDR, 2013).

As urbanization mirrors economic growth, rapid urban development by itself contributes to the concentration of risk in hazard-exposed locations . However, in most low and middle-income countries urban development is also usually characterized by highly unequal access to urban space, infrastructure, services and security. 24 (UNISDR, 2015).

The result is socially segregated urban development, which in turn generates new patterns of both extensive and intensive disaster risk . Low-income households in particular are often forced to occupy hazard-exposed areas with low land values, deficient or non-existent infrastructure and social protection, and high levels of environmental degradation (UNISDR, 2015 ).

Many of the costs of disasters in urban areas are spread across communities and sectors and so become shared costs, making it difficult to attribute responsibility.(UNISDR, 2013)

An enormous volume of capital is expected to flow into urban development in the coming decades. The future of disaster risk reduction will depend heavily on ensuring that this future urban development is risk-sensitive (UNISDR, 2015 ).

Urban land cover is expected increase by 56% to 310% the current cover between 2000 and 2030. Only 40% of the area expected to be urbanised by 2030 has been built.(reference 26 – UNISDR, 2015)

Essential to building the resilience of urban areas and ensuring they are developed in a risk-sensitive manner is the implementation of effective governance.

Opportunity for building resilience

As with all the underlying risk drivers, because badly planned and managed urbanisations is so closely linked to a number of other risk drivers, it must be addressed in combination with reducing these other drivers of risk.

A new wave of urbanisation is unfolding in hazard-exposed countries and with it, new opportunities for resilient investment emerge.

As a business sector, urban development involves a wide range of stakeholders. Decisions to reduce risk could take place at a number of points, from pre-project design to post-completion. Approaches for lessening and managing disaster risk include investment and public regulation, resilient infrastructure development, risk transfer and more effective risk governance. In particularly new approaches to sustainable urban development provide opportunities for partnerships between businesses and with the public sector (UNISDR, 2013 ).

The 2015 Global Assessment Report highlights that higher-income countries and some larger cities in middle-income countries have made sound progress in building risk sensitive urban development. Some of the most promising developments in recent years are cases where cities have been able to regain control of their planning and management and to strengthen their urban governance through innovative partnerships between local governments, households and communities (UNISDR, 2015 ).

Many low and middle-income countries have lacked the capacities to plan and manage urban development in an appropriate and risk-sensitive way, in particular in small urban centres. As a consequence, urban disaster risks have grown at a faster rate than they have been reduced.(UNISDR, 2015)

Urbanisation can also create opportunities for enhancing resilience at the local level; households may have access to goods and services they did not otherwise have access to when in rural areas. The opening of new urban markets may also improve the viability of rural livelihoods, increase household incomes and contribute to a reduction in both vulnerability and poverty – through access to sell agricultural products or engage in non-agricultural work. However, urbanisation can also increase vulnerability and lower resilience in rural areas through the displacement of urban pollution, waste and over-extraction of water resources onto rural communities. Moving to urban areas can also radically restructure social and gender relations, meaning that potential changes in risk affect men and women, the old and young, the wealthy and the poor in different ways.
