Globalization increases the value of assets at risk

While globalization has spurred economic growth it has also dramatically increased the value of assets at risk. The value of produced capital in East Asia and the Pacific, for example, more than doubled from US$4.6 trillion in 1995 to US$10 trillion in 2005. The proportion of global GDP exposed to tropical cyclones increased from 3.6 percent to 4.3 percent since 1970. Globally US$71 trillion of assets are exposed to one-in-250 year earthquakes.

GAR 2013

Produced capital stock in Southeast Asia and cyclone wind hazard (50 years return period)© GAR 2013

Key Concepts

Exposure

[Exposure refers to] people, property, systems, or other elements present in hazard zones that are thereby subject to potential losses.

UNISDR 2009

Exposure is one of the three components of disaster risk:
Risk = Hazard x Exposure x Vulnerability

Port cities with high exposure and vulnerability to climate extremes

GAR 2013

Maritime transport handles over 80 percent of the volume of global trade and accounts for over 70 percent of its value. Since 1970, global seaborne trade has expanded by an annual average of 3.1 percent and has doubled in the last 30 years. Port cities are a vital nexus in global supply chains. In 2005, 13 of the 20 most populated cities in the world were port cities. Many of these are exposed to flooding and storms. An analysis of a sample of 136 port cities with populations of more than 1 million highlighted that currently North America has the highest volume of exposed economic assets and Asia the largest proportion of exposed population.
